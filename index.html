Every Superhero Needs an Origin Story

I have one too.

I've been in IT for a while. Thought I was going to be an academic computer scientist. Surprise!

“Life is what happens to you while you are busy making other plans.”

(click buttons to reveal chapters)

Got yanked out of the academic career path into “Wall Street” to apply computing expertise and analytic skills to evaluating “hot” emerging technologies, companies, trends, and business opportunities.

Made investors money and moved markets. Participated in IPOs and witnessed the birth of the first real “quant” investing.

Most of all, learned the things that really drive human and organizational change. Not “better mousetraps,” but better opportunities.

Within a few years, my firm shifted from financial to industrial clients to capture the epic rise of networked computing. It started with ① networked PCs, client/server apps, Unix workstations and servers, Windows, distributed computing, RISC CPUs, clustered HPTC, “open standards,” COTS replacing bespoke, data warehousing and VLDB. I tracked right into ② the internet, Java, the world-wide web, e-business, x86 remade, and the birth of open source. Then ③ scale-out computing, Big Iron Unix, grid, SOA, web services, and the start of programmable “infrastructure as software.” Then ④ everything web, agile, virtualization, dynamic languages, modular servers, a data center and power/energy revolution, cloud, deep computing, “big data” and analytics, rich internet apps, widespread data integration, and the end of IT as a box business. I’ve eagerly tracked right into ⑤ mobile ascendent, everything-as-a-service, agile DevOps as law, the normalization of continuously integrated and delivered apps and network businesses, pervasive broadband, the rise of JavaScript and node.js, and the breakout of data science and machine learning. Even so, I’ve left out the half of it, and remain eager for ⑥ what comes next.

Whatever happened in the networked computing revolution, I was there on the front lines, as or before it went mainstream. Served as analyst and advisor at the highest levels possible. Consulted and retained by many of the world’s largest IT developers, investors, and consumers. Called upon by business executives, corporate teams, government agencies, distinguished engineers and research fellows alike. I traveled the world, keynoted events, guided major company and development initiatives, was widely quoted by the tech and business press, sparked mergers and acquisitions, helped answer questions in times of chaos and transition, and served as expert in epic legal and procurement disputes.

Phenomenal as my advisory career was, I still wanted to be *more* involved. Being an analyst and consultant brings you into exciting projects, efforts, and situations—often at the highest levels, and in their pivotal moments. But however key your insights and guidance, however much you get to structure the plan, choose the tools, or figure out how to rescue a bad situation, there’s limited opportunity for “skin in the game.” Engagements are transitory. You help a team choose or correct its path, but they’re the ones that get to “run the play.”

By 2010, I had “done it all.” I could continue acing that course for another thirty-something years, but as an analyst and consultant, there’s nothing left to prove. No more mountains to climb. What’s the fun in doing the same-ol’, same-ol’? Time for a new horizon.

In 2011, I fundamentally refocused on building apps and operating services. Had actually never stopped building things, having worked on significant internal IT projects and open source contributions all along. But hands-on became my primary responsibility. Python, JavaScript, repos, cloud instances, and DevOps became my workaday companions. Stack Overflow and code deep-dives became my constant reading. Still work with SVPs and CTOs, but my clients have greatly diversified into everyday developers, operators, app owners, domain specialists, and students. I’ve rekindled my enthusiasm for mentoring, especially through codementor.io.
