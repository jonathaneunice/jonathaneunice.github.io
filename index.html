Why DevOps?

DevOps is a force-multiplier. It provides the historic opportunity to connect all phases of IT, from the imagination of designers and architects through the meticulous monitoring and care of production services—in other words, the power and levers to "improve the way things are done."

Yes it includes all the frustration of "mechanic work." Yes it requires bringing together invariably-recalcitrant people, processes, and tools. There are silos to be worked around, ugly fixes to be improved, and constant time pressure. And yes, you get blamed for everything that doesn't work, no matter who picked it, wrote it, owns it, or currently runs it—no matter how long it's been a problem.

"Other than that, Mrs. Lincoln, how was the play?"

I admire, take solace from the travails of, and learn from other difficult fields such as fire-fighting, motion picture production, and America's get-to-the-moon program. When there are dozens or hundreds of problems ahead of you every day, nothing suffices but getting into the fight. Fight high and low, strategic and tactical, left and right. Focus on *this* problem right in front of you the next hour, then when that's tamped down, move on to the next dozen things that needed fixed two weeks, months, or years ago. And keep enough in the tank to fight the long-term, systemic problems that no one else has imagined can yet be fixed.

Frustrations notwithstanding, DevOps is a wonderful field that epitomizes the power of integration, networked resources, and a positive opportunity to make things right. Even the largest knots can be untied, if you're systematic and relentless. That constant challenge is matched by the constant opportunity to fix, to improve, and to create leverage.
