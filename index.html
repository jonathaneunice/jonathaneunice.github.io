I've been in IT for a while. Thought I was going to be an academic computer scientist. Surprise!

Got yanked out of the academic career path into "Wall Street" to apply computing expertise and analytic skills to evaluating "hot" emerging technologies, companies, trends, and business opportunities. Made investors money and moved markets in that capacity. Participated in IPOs and witnessed the birth of the first real "quant" investing. Most of all, learned the things that really drive human and organizational change. Not "better mousetraps," but better opportunities.

Within a few years, Wall Street had evolved. My firm shifted from financial to industrial clients to capture the "client/server" wave—the epic rise of networked PCs, Unix workstations and servers, Windows, distributed computing, RISC CPUs, clustered HPC, "open standards," COTS, data warehousing and VLDB. I tracked right into the second wave with the internet, open source, and e-business, third wave with scale-out computing, Java, grid, SOA, web services, and fourth wave with everything web, agile, virtualization, modular servers, a datacenter and power/energy revolution, cloud, rich internet apps, IoT, DevOps, and the end of IT as a box business. Even so, I've left out the half of it.

Whatever happened in the networked computing revolution, I was there on the front lines, as or before it went mainstream. Served as analyst and advisor at the highest levels possible. Consulted and retained by many of the world's largest IT developers, investors, and consumers. Called upon by business executives, corporate teams, government agencies, distinguished engineers and research fellows alike. I traveled the world, keynoted events, guided major company and development initiatives, was widely quoted by the tech and business press, sparked mergers and acquisitions, helped answer questions in times of chaos and transition, and served as expert in epic legal and procurement disputes.

Phenomenal as my advisory career was, I still wanted to be *more* involved. Being an analyst and consultant brings you into exciting projects, efforts, and situations—often at the highest levels and/or in their pivotal moments. But however key your insights and guidance, however much you get to structure the plan, choose the tools, or figure out how to rescue a bad situation, there's limited "skin in the game." Engagements are transitory. You help a team choose or correct its path, but they're the ones that get to "run the play."

By 2010, I had "done it all." I could continue acing that course for another thirty-something years, but as an analyst and consultant, there's nothing left to prove. No more mountains to climb. What's the fun in doing the same-old, same-old? Time for a new horizon.

In 2011, I fundamentally refocused on building apps and operating services. Had actually never stopped building, having worked on significant internal IT projects and open source contributions all along. But hands-on became my primary responsibility. Python, JavaScript, repos, cloud instances, and DevOps became my closest workaday companions. Stack Overflow and code deep-dives became my constant reading materials. Still work with SVPs and CTOs, but my clients have greatly diversified into everyday developers, operators, app owners, domain specialists, and students. I've rekindled my enthusiasm for mentoring, especially through codementor.io.
